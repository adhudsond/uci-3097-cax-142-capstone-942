Employee Offboarding (IT De-provisioning)

1. HR notifies IT of a departure by email, sometimes on the employee's last day or even after they've left.
2. An IT technician reads the email and starts a manual checklist kept in a personal document.
3. The technician disables the Active Directory account, but only when they get around to it that day.
4. The technician manually removes the user from email distribution lists one at a time.
5. The technician revokes access to cloud apps (CRM, file storage, code repos) by logging into each admin console separately.
6. Company hardware (laptop, phone, badge) is collected ad hoc; sometimes it's noted in the asset spreadsheet, sometimes not.
7. The departed employee's files and mailbox are left in place "just in case," with no defined retention period.
8. Shared passwords the employee knew are rarely rotated.
9. There is no final confirmation step verifying that every access point was actually closed.

Pain points: notification is late and informal, de-provisioning is slow and
manual across many disconnected systems, access is frequently left active after
departure (a serious security risk), hardware return is inconsistently tracked,
and there is no audit trail proving access was fully revoked.
